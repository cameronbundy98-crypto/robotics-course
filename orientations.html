Rotation Matrices

What Is an Orientation?

A coordinate frame {B}\{B\} attached to a rigid body is described by three orthonormal axes 𝐛1,𝐛2,𝐛3\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \mathbf{b}_3 expressed in a fixed world frame {W}\{W\}.

The rotation matrix WRB∈ℝ3×3{}^W R_B \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 3} stacks those axes as columns:

WRB=[|||𝐛1𝐛2𝐛3|||]{}^W R_B = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & | \\ \mathbf{b}_1 & \mathbf{b}_2 & \mathbf{b}_3 \\ | & | & | \end{bmatrix}

Each column is a unit vector of {B}\{B\} written in {W}\{W\} coordinates. Reading columns gives “where the body axes point.”

The Special Orthogonal Group SO(3)

A rotation matrix satisfies two conditions:

  1. Orthonormality — RTR=I3R^T R = I_3, equivalently R−1=RTR^{-1} = R^T
  2. Proper rotation — det(R)=+1\det(R) = +1

The set of all such matrices forms the Lie group SO(3):

SO(3)={R∈ℝ3×3∣RTR=I,detR=1}\mathrm{SO}(3) = \bigl\{R \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times 3} \mid R^T R = I,\ \det R = 1\bigr\}

SO(3) is a group: closed under multiplication, has identity II, every element has an inverse R−1=RTR^{-1} = R^T, and multiplication is associative.

Basic Rotations

Rotation by angle θ\theta about the xx-axis:

Rx(θ)=[1000cosθ−sinθ0sinθcosθ]R_x(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ 0 & \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}

About the yy-axis:

Ry(θ)=[cosθ0sinθ010−sinθ0cosθ]R_y(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & 0 & \sin\theta \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ -\sin\theta & 0 & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}

About the zz-axis:

Rz(θ)=[cosθ−sinθ0sinθcosθ0001]R_z(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}

Rotating Vectors

If 𝐩B\mathbf{p}^B are the coordinates of a point in frame {B}\{B\}, its coordinates in {W}\{W\} are:

𝐩W=WRB𝐩B\mathbf{p}^W = {}^W R_B\, \mathbf{p}^B

To go the other direction — from world to body — use R−1=RTR^{-1} = R^T:

𝐩B=WRBT𝐩W\mathbf{p}^B = {}^W R_B^T\, \mathbf{p}^W

Active vs. passive interpretation. The same matrix RR can either rotate a vector (active) or change the frame in which it is expressed (passive). We use the passive interpretation: RR maps body coordinates to world coordinates.

Composing Rotations

Two sequential rotations compose by matrix multiplication:

WRC=WRB⋅BRC{}^W R_C = {}^W R_B \cdot {}^B R_C

Order matters — rotation is not commutative:

Rx(α)Rz(β)≠Rz(β)Rx(α)in generalR_x(\alpha)\,R_z(\beta) \neq R_z(\beta)\,R_x(\alpha) \quad \text{in general}

Read the composition right to left: WRC{}^W R_C first applies BRC{}^B R_C (body → intermediate), then WRB{}^W R_B (intermediate → world).

Euler Angles

Euler’s Theorem

Euler’s Rotation Theorem: Any orientation of a rigid body can be achieved by a single rotation about some axis through the origin.

A practical consequence: we can parameterize any R∈SO(3)R \in \mathrm{SO}(3) with just three numbers.

Euler angles do this by decomposing RR into three successive rotations about (body or world) axes:

R=Ri(α)Rj(β)Rk(γ),i,j,k∈{x,y,z},i≠j≠iR = R_{i}(\alpha)\, R_{j}(\beta)\, R_{k}(\gamma), \quad i,j,k \in \{x, y, z\},\ i \neq j \neq i

ZYX Convention (Yaw–Pitch–Roll)

The most common convention in aerospace and robotics:

R=Rz(ψ)Ry(θ)Rx(ϕ)R = R_z(\psi)\, R_y(\theta)\, R_x(\phi)

Angle Symbol Rotation Axis Name
1st ψ\psi zz Yaw
2nd θ\theta yy Pitch
3rd ϕ\phi xx Roll

Applied right to left: first roll about xx, then pitch about (new) yy, then yaw about (new) zz.

This is an intrinsic sequence — each rotation is about the body’s current axis. Equivalently, the same result is obtained by extrinsic rotations in the reversed order: Rx(ϕ)R_x(\phi) then Ry(θ)R_y(\theta) then Rz(ψ)R_z(\psi) about fixed world axes.

Expanded ZYX Matrix

Multiplying out Rz(ψ)Ry(θ)Rx(ϕ)R_z(\psi)\,R_y(\theta)\,R_x(\phi):

R=[cψcθcψsθsϕ−sψcϕcψsθcϕ+sψsϕsψcθsψsθsϕ+cψcϕsψsθcϕ−cψsϕ−sθcθsϕcθcϕ]R = \begin{bmatrix} c_\psi c_\theta & c_\psi s_\theta s_\phi - s_\psi c_\phi & c_\psi s_\theta c_\phi + s_\psi s_\phi \\ s_\psi c_\theta & s_\psi s_\theta s_\phi + c_\psi c_\phi & s_\psi s_\theta c_\phi - c_\psi s_\phi \\ -s_\theta & c_\theta s_\phi & c_\theta c_\phi \end{bmatrix}

where cα≡cosαc_\alpha \equiv \cos\alpha, sα≡sinαs_\alpha \equiv \sin\alpha.

Extracting angles from RR (when cosθ≠0\cos\theta \neq 0):

θ=arcsin(−R31),ϕ=atan2(R32,R33),ψ=atan2(R21,R11)\theta = \arcsin(-R_{31}), \quad \phi = \mathrm{atan2}(R_{32}, R_{33}), \quad \psi = \mathrm{atan2}(R_{21}, R_{11})

Gimbal Lock

When θ=±90°\theta = \pm 90° (pitch straight up or down), the matrix simplifies and ϕ\phi and ψ\psi become indistinguishable — one degree of freedom is lost.

Gimbal lock is not a physical constraint — the body can still rotate freely. It is a singularity of the parameterization: infinitely many (ϕ,ψ)(\phi, \psi) pairs map to the same RR when θ=±90°\theta = \pm 90°.

Practical consequences:

  • Numerical instability near θ=±90°\theta = \pm 90°
  • Cannot smoothly interpolate through singularity
  • Discontinuous angle extraction

Euler Angle Pros and Cons

Advantages

  • Minimal — only 3 numbers
  • Intuitive for human interpretation
  • Easy to specify end-effector poses
  • Singularity-free away from gimbal lock

Disadvantages

  • Gimbal lock at certain configurations
  • Many conventions (12+ in use)
  • Not unique (multiple angle sets → same RR)
  • Poor for interpolation near singularity
  • Velocity kinematics require a Jacobian

Quaternions

Motivation

Can we find a compact, singularity-free representation?

Axis-angle representation: any rotation is a rotation by angle θ\theta about unit axis 𝐮̂=(ux,uy,uz)T\hat{\mathbf{u}} = (u_x, u_y, u_z)^T, giving the Rodrigues formula:

R=I+sinθ[𝐮̂]×+(1−cosθ)[𝐮̂]×2R = I + \sin\theta\, [\hat{\mathbf{u}}]_\times + (1-\cos\theta)\, [\hat{\mathbf{u}}]_\times^2

where [𝐮̂]×[\hat{\mathbf{u}}]_\times is the skew-symmetric cross-product matrix:

[𝐮̂]×=[0−uzuyuz0−ux−uyux0][\hat{\mathbf{u}}]_\times = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -u_z & u_y \\ u_z & 0 & -u_x \\ -u_y & u_x & 0 \end{bmatrix}

Axis-angle still has a singularity at θ=0\theta = 0 (axis is undefined). Quaternions resolve this by encoding both axis and angle algebraically.

Unit Quaternions

A quaternion is a 4-tuple:

𝐪=w+x𝐢+y𝐣+z𝐤=(w,𝐯),𝐯=(x,y,z)T\mathbf{q} = w + x\,\mathbf{i} + y\,\mathbf{j} + z\,\mathbf{k} = (w,\, \mathbf{v}), \quad \mathbf{v} = (x,y,z)^T

For rotations we use unit quaternions — those with ∥𝐪∥=1\|\mathbf{q}\| = 1:

w2+x2+y2+z2=1w^2 + x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1

Encoding axis-angle (𝐮̂,θ)(\hat{\mathbf{u}}, \theta) as a quaternion:

𝐪=(cosθ2,𝐮̂sinθ2)\mathbf{q} = \left(\cos\frac{\theta}{2},\; \hat{\mathbf{u}}\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\right)

Double cover: 𝐪\mathbf{q} and −𝐪-\mathbf{q} encode the same rotation. Unit quaternions live on S3⊂ℝ4S^3 \subset \mathbb{R}^4, and SO(3) is the quotient S3/{±1}S^3/\{\pm 1\}.

Quaternion Algebra

The imaginary units satisfy 𝐢2=𝐣2=𝐤2=𝐢𝐣𝐤=−1\mathbf{i}^2 = \mathbf{j}^2 = \mathbf{k}^2 = \mathbf{ijk} = -1, giving:

𝐢𝐣=𝐤,𝐣𝐤=𝐢,𝐤𝐢=𝐣\mathbf{i}\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k},\quad \mathbf{j}\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i},\quad \mathbf{k}\mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j}

Quaternion product (w1,𝐯1)⊗(w2,𝐯2)(w_1, \mathbf{v}_1) \otimes (w_2, \mathbf{v}_2):

𝐪1⊗𝐪2=(w1w2−𝐯1⋅𝐯2,w1𝐯2+w2𝐯1+𝐯1×𝐯2)\mathbf{q}_1 \otimes \mathbf{q}_2 = \bigl(w_1 w_2 - \mathbf{v}_1\cdot\mathbf{v}_2,\; w_1\mathbf{v}_2 + w_2\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_1 \times \mathbf{v}_2\bigr)

Conjugate and inverse (for unit quaternions):

𝐪*=(w,−𝐯),𝐪−1=𝐪*(since ∥𝐪∥=1)\mathbf{q}^* = (w, -\mathbf{v}), \qquad \mathbf{q}^{-1} = \mathbf{q}^* \quad (\text{since } \|\mathbf{q}\|=1)

Rotating a Vector

To rotate a 3-vector 𝐩\mathbf{p} by quaternion 𝐪\mathbf{q}, embed 𝐩\mathbf{p} as a pure quaternion 𝐩̃=(0,𝐩)\tilde{\mathbf{p}} = (0, \mathbf{p}) and compute:

𝐩̃′=𝐪⊗𝐩̃⊗𝐪*\tilde{\mathbf{p}}' = \mathbf{q} \otimes \tilde{\mathbf{p}} \otimes \mathbf{q}^*

The result 𝐩̃′=(0,𝐩′)\tilde{\mathbf{p}}' = (0, \mathbf{p}') is again a pure quaternion, and 𝐩′\mathbf{p}' is the rotated vector.

This is equivalent to 𝐩′=R𝐩\mathbf{p}' = R\,\mathbf{p} where RR is the rotation matrix corresponding to 𝐪\mathbf{q}. The sandwich product 𝐪(⋅)𝐪*\mathbf{q}(\cdot)\mathbf{q}^* preserves vector length.

Quaternion to Rotation Matrix

Given 𝐪=(w,x,y,z)\mathbf{q} = (w, x, y, z), the equivalent rotation matrix is:

R(𝐪)=[1−2(y2+z2)2(xy−wz)2(xz+wy)2(xy+wz)1−2(x2+z2)2(yz−wx)2(xz−wy)2(yz+wx)1−2(x2+y2)]R(\mathbf{q}) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 - 2(y^2+z^2) & 2(xy - wz) & 2(xz + wy) \\ 2(xy + wz) & 1 - 2(x^2+z^2) & 2(yz - wx) \\ 2(xz - wy) & 2(yz + wx) & 1 - 2(x^2+y^2) \end{bmatrix}

This follows directly from expanding 𝐪⊗𝐩̃⊗𝐪*\mathbf{q} \otimes \tilde{\mathbf{p}} \otimes \mathbf{q}^* and collecting terms.

Composing Rotations

Two sequential rotations compose by quaternion multiplication:

𝐪AC=𝐪AB⊗𝐪BC\mathbf{q}_{AC} = \mathbf{q}_{AB} \otimes \mathbf{q}_{BC}

This mirrors matrix composition — read right to left.

Numerical drift: After many multiplications, ∥𝐪∥\|\mathbf{q}\| may drift from 1 due to floating-point errors. Re-normalize periodically: 𝐪←𝐪∥𝐪∥\mathbf{q} \leftarrow \frac{\mathbf{q}}{\|\mathbf{q}\|}

Spherical Linear Interpolation (SLERP)

Quaternions enable smooth interpolation between two orientations 𝐪0\mathbf{q}_0 and 𝐪1\mathbf{q}_1:

slerp(𝐪0,𝐪1,t)=𝐪0(𝐪0−1⊗𝐪1)t,t∈[0,1]\mathrm{slerp}(\mathbf{q}_0, \mathbf{q}_1, t) = \mathbf{q}_0 \left(\mathbf{q}_0^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{q}_1\right)^t, \quad t \in [0, 1]

Or equivalently, via the angle Ω=arccos(𝐪0⋅𝐪1)\Omega = \arccos(\mathbf{q}_0 \cdot \mathbf{q}_1):

slerp(𝐪0,𝐪1,t)=sin((1−t)Ω)sinΩ𝐪0+sin(tΩ)sinΩ𝐪1\mathrm{slerp}(\mathbf{q}_0, \mathbf{q}_1, t) = \frac{\sin((1-t)\Omega)}{\sin\Omega}\,\mathbf{q}_0 + \frac{\sin(t\Omega)}{\sin\Omega}\,\mathbf{q}_1

SLERP traces a geodesic on S3S^3 — the shortest path between two orientations. Simple linear interpolation of Euler angles does not give the shortest path and can produce unexpected trajectories.

SLERP: Numerical Example

Setup: interpolate from identity to 90° rotation about ẑ\hat{z}, evaluate at t=0.5t = 0.5.

𝐪0=(1,0,0,0),𝐪1=(cos45°,0,0,sin45°)=(22,0,0,22)\mathbf{q}_0 = (1,\;0,\;0,\;0), \qquad \mathbf{q}_1 = \left(\cos 45°,\;0,\;0,\;\sin 45°\right) = \left(\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\;0,\;0,\;\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right)

Step 1 — find Ω\Omega:

cosΩ=𝐪0⋅𝐪1=22⇒Ω=45°\cos\Omega = \mathbf{q}_0 \cdot \mathbf{q}_1 = \tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \;\Longrightarrow\; \Omega = 45°

Ω=45°\Omega = 45° is half the rotation angle (90°). This is a direct consequence of the half-angle encoding w=cos(θ/2)w = \cos(\theta/2).

Step 2 — evaluate at t=0.5t = 0.5:

slerp=sin22.5°sin45°𝐪0+sin22.5°sin45°𝐪1≈0.541(𝐪0+𝐪1)\mathrm{slerp} = \frac{\sin 22.5°}{\sin 45°}\,\mathbf{q}_0 + \frac{\sin 22.5°}{\sin 45°}\,\mathbf{q}_1 \approx 0.541\,(\mathbf{q}_0 + \mathbf{q}_1)

=0.541(1.707,0,0,0.707)≈(0.924,0,0,0.383)= 0.541\,(1.707,\;0,\;0,\;0.707) \approx (0.924,\;0,\;0,\;0.383)

SLERP: Decoding the Result

𝐪0.5≈(0.924⏟w,0,0,0.383⏟vz)\mathbf{q}_{0.5} \approx (\underbrace{0.924}_{w},\;0,\;0,\;\underbrace{0.383}_{v_z})

Decode the rotation angle:

w=cosθ2=0.924=cos22.5°⇒θ=45°w = \cos\!\tfrac{\theta}{2} = 0.924 = \cos 22.5° \;\Longrightarrow\; \theta = 45°

Decode the rotation axis:

𝐮̂=𝐯∥𝐯∥=(0,0,0.383)0.383=ẑ\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|} = \frac{(0,\;0,\;0.383)}{0.383} = \hat{z}

So 𝐪0.5\mathbf{q}_{0.5} is a 45° rotation about ẑ\hat{z} — exactly halfway between 0° and 90°, on the same axis. ✓

This holds generally: SLERP between two rotations about the same axis stays on that axis and scales the angle linearly. For rotations about different axes, the intermediate axis traces a curved path on S3S^3.

SLERP: Where Is the Axis Computed?

In the multiplicative form slerp=𝐪0(𝐪0−1⊗𝐪1)t\mathrm{slerp} = \mathbf{q}_0 \left(\mathbf{q}_0^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{q}_1\right)^t, the axis is explicit:

𝐪rel=𝐪0−1⊗𝐪1←relative rotation from 𝐪0 to 𝐪1\mathbf{q}_{rel} = \mathbf{q}_0^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{q}_1 \quad \leftarrow \text{relative rotation from } \mathbf{q}_0 \text{ to } \mathbf{q}_1

The axis of 𝐪rel\mathbf{q}_{rel} (the normalized vector part) is the axis about which SLERP rotates. Raising to the power tt scales only the angle, leaving the axis unchanged:

𝐪relt corresponds to angle tα about 𝐮̂(𝐪rel encodes angle α about 𝐮̂)\mathbf{q}_{rel}^t \;\text{ corresponds to angle } t\alpha \text{ about } \hat{\mathbf{u}} \quad (\mathbf{q}_{rel} \text{ encodes angle } \alpha \text{ about } \hat{\mathbf{u}})

In our example (𝐪0=\mathbf{q}_0 = identity):

𝐪rel=𝐪0−1⊗𝐪1=𝐪1=(22,0,0,22)⇒𝐮̂=ẑ\mathbf{q}_{rel} = \mathbf{q}_0^{-1} \otimes \mathbf{q}_1 = \mathbf{q}_1 = \left(\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\;0,\;0,\;\tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\right) \;\Rightarrow\; \hat{\mathbf{u}} = \hat{z}

In the dot-product formula, the axis is not extracted explicitly — it is preserved implicitly because the weighted sum α0𝐪0+α1𝐪1\alpha_0\,\mathbf{q}_0 + \alpha_1\,\mathbf{q}_1 stays on the great circle whose tangent direction encodes 𝐮̂\hat{\mathbf{u}}.

When to Use Which

Rotation Matrices

Best for:

  • Deriving equations (Jacobians, dynamics)
  • Linear algebra manipulations
  • Expressing velocity relations
  • Composing many transforms analytically

Euler Angles

Best for:

  • User interfaces / teleoperation
  • Specifying initial conditions
  • Small-angle approximations
  • Aerospace attitude display

Quaternions

Best for:

  • Real-time control / estimation
  • Motion planning & interpolation
  • Simulation & numerical integration
  • State estimation (EKF, UKF)

In practice: store orientations as quaternions, display as Euler angles, derive equations with rotation matrices.

Going backwards:

𝐪→R(𝐪)R→atan2(ϕ,θ,ψ)\mathbf{q} \xrightarrow{R(\mathbf{q})} R \xrightarrow{\mathrm{atan2}} (\phi, \theta, \psi)

The Lie algebra 𝔰𝔬(3)\mathfrak{so}(3) (skew-symmetric matrices) relates to all three via the exponential map:

R=e[𝛚]×,𝐪≈(1,𝛚2) for small ∥𝛚∥R = e^{[\boldsymbol{\omega}]_\times}, \qquad \mathbf{q} \approx \left(1,\, \tfrac{\boldsymbol{\omega}}{2}\right) \text{ for small } \|\boldsymbol{\omega}\|

The vector 𝛚∈ℝ3\boldsymbol{\omega} \in \mathbb{R}^3 is the rotation vector (axis times angle). It is the minimal, globally-defined representation — but it too has a singularity at ∥𝛚∥=2πk\|\boldsymbol{\omega}\| = 2\pi k.
