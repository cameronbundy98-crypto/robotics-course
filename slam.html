The Localization Problem

A robot is typically unable to measure its position.

Instead, it can measure its position in relation to objects in the world.

The Fundamental Challenge

Can’t use Kalman Filter directly to estimate location when we don’t have a map of objects in the world.

Without a map, we don’t know what to expect as measurement in a state (no sensor model).

Key insight: If we don’t have a map, we need to build one while simultaneously figuring out where we are.

SLAM: The Core Idea

SLAM tries to simultaneously make sense of:

  • Where we think we are (localization)
  • What we think we should be seeing (mapping)

Basic idea: Make the map a part of the state.

We can build measurement and motion models for this expanded state.

2D SLAM: Non-rotating Robot

State Definition

The robot at (xr,yr)(x_r, y_r) measures the location of landmarks li=(lix,liy)l_i = (l_{ix}, l_{iy}) in its frame of reference, which is aligned with the world axis.

State vector: x=[xryrl1xl1yl2xl2y⋮lnlxlnly] x = \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \\ l_{1x} \\ l_{1y} \\ l_{2x} \\ l_{2y} \\ \vdots \\ l_{n_l x} \\ l_{n_l y} \end{bmatrix}

where there are nln_l landmarks.

Motion Model

The landmarks are assumed stationary, with robot moving according to: [xryr]t+1=[xryr]t+ut+vt \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \end{bmatrix}_{t+1} = \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \end{bmatrix}_{t} + u_t + v_t

System matrices:

AA is (2+2nl)×(2+2nl)(2 + 2n_l) \times (2 + 2n_l), the only non-zero elements being: A1,1=1,A2,2=1A_{1,1} = 1, \quad A_{2,2} = 1

BB is (2+2nl)×2(2 + 2n_l) \times 2, the only non-zero elements being: B1,1=1,B2,2=1B_{1,1} = 1, \quad B_{2,2} = 1

Measurement Model

If the robot sees all landmarks in its frame, its measurement is: y=[l1x−xrl1y−yrl2x−xrl2y−yr⋮] y = \begin{bmatrix} l_{1x} - x_r \\ l_{1y} - y_r \\ l_{2x} - x_r \\ l_{2y} - y_r \\ \vdots \end{bmatrix}

Measurement Matrix

CC is 2nl×(2+2nl)2n_l \times (2 + 2n_l):

C=[−101000…000−10100…00−100010…000−10001…00⋮⋮⋮−100000…100−10000…01] C = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 & \dots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & & & & & \vdots & & \\ -1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \dots & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}

Performance Analysis

Performance when robot always sees all landmarks:

  • The estimated mean position of each landmark is quite close to the actual landmark position, relative to other landmarks
  • The uncertainty in the landmark position is identical to the uncertainty in initial position, in the long run
    • The robot can treat the landmark as lying within the 3σ3\sigma ellipse of the landmark mean
    • To avoid collision with high probability, the 3σ3\sigma ellipse corresponding to its position should lie outside the landmark’s ellipse
  • The difference between the robot’s true and estimated location depends on sensor accuracy

Extended Kalman Filter

Nonlinear Systems

For linear systems with Gaussian additive noise, the Kalman filter provides the least uncertain estimate.

When the system is nonlinear: xt+1=f(xt,ut)+vtyt=h(xt)+wt \begin{aligned} x_{t+1} &= f(x_t, u_t) + v_t \\ y_t &= h(x_t) + w_t \end{aligned}

Linearization Approach

Use linearization:

At=∂f∂x|x=μt,Bt=∂f∂u|u=ut,Ct=∂h∂x|x=μtpred A_t = \left. \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \right|_{x = \mu_t}, \quad B_t = \left. \frac{\partial f}{\partial u} \right|_{u = u_t}, \quad C_t = \left. \frac{\partial h}{\partial x} \right|_{x = \mu_t^{pred}}

A blind application of Kalman filter updates using AtA_t, BtB_t, and CtC_t is not guaranteed to work but often does well in practice.

2D SLAM: Rotating Robot

Motion Model

The inputs are the speed ss of the robot along its current heading direction, and the angular velocity ω\omega: ut=[stωt] u_t = \begin{bmatrix} s_t \\ \omega_t \end{bmatrix}

The motion model is now nonlinear: [xryrθr]t+1=[xryrθr]t+[stcosθrstsinθrωt]+vt=f(xt,ut) \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \\ \theta_r \end{bmatrix}_{t+1} = \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \\ \theta_r \end{bmatrix}_{t} + \begin{bmatrix} s_t \cos \theta_r \\ s_t \sin \theta_r \\ \omega_t \end{bmatrix} + v_t = f(x_t, u_t)

Linearized Motion Model

We linearize to get part of the AA and BB matrices:

[xryrθr]t+1=[10−sinθrst01cosθrst001][xryrθr]t+[cosθr0sinθr001]ut+vt \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \\ \theta_r \end{bmatrix}_{t+1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -\sin \theta_r \, s_t \\ 0 & 1 & \cos \theta_r \, s_t \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_r \\ y_r \\ \theta_r \end{bmatrix}_{t} + \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta_r & 0 \\ \sin \theta_r & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} u_t + v_t

Measurement Model

The position of a landmark lil_i in the robot’s frame becomes:

yi=[cosθr−sinθrsinθrcosθr][lix−xrliy−yr] y_i = \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta_r & -\sin \theta_r \\ \sin \theta_r & \cos \theta_r \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} l_{ix} - x_r \\ l_{iy} - y_r \end{bmatrix}

The full measurement yy corresponds to stacking the yiy_is.

Nonlinear Measurement

The measurement model is nonlinear: y=h(x)≠Cxy = h(x) \neq Cx for any matrix CC.

As mentioned earlier, we linearize: C=∂h∂x|x=μtpred C = \left. \frac{\partial h}{\partial x} \right|_{x = \mu_t^{pred}}

Practical Considerations

Limited Sensing

We’ve assumed that we can see all landmarks at once.

In practice, we can only see nearby landmarks.

Data Association Problem

This limitation creates a data association problem when landmarks look similar to each other. For example, the entry to an office cubicle.
